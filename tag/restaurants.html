Restaurant Grades in NYC¶

Motivations and Background¶

Every year, the New York City Health Department inspects food service establishments (that includes restaurants, cafes, donuts shops, etc.) in the area to monitor compliance with city and state food regulations.

Every restaurant gets inspected at least once a year, unannounced. The result of an inspection is a violation score, higher points meaning greater violations. Scores of less than 14 gets an A immediately, scores between 14 and 27 get a B (or Pending Grade if the grade is to be challenged) and scores greater than 28 get a C (or Pending Grade if the grade is to be challenged). If there is a critical public hazard at the time of inspection, then the hazard must be immediately corrected or the inspected establishment will be asked to shut down by the city regulators. In that case, the score or grade is not given.

More information about the inspection process can be found here.

Beginning July 2010, the city regulations require restaurants to publicly disclose their health grades at the premise of the business where it can be easily seen, this letter grade could signficantly influence the decision of customers on where to choose to dine, concerning the health sanitation issues. Furthermore, these inspections can result in the city revoking the permits to operate of the inspected businesses. Using our dataset, we can carry out an exploratory data analysis and visualization to see any interesting trends and patterns of restaurants and health inspections in NYC over the years.

Dataset¶

Our dataset contains nearly 400,000 inspection records of food establishments in NYC area. The data was downloaded from Kaggle website and can be accessed here. The column definitions are extracted from official city of New York website and can be found here.

The column definitions are as follows:

  • 'CAMIS', an unique identifier for the entity (restaurant)
  • 'DBA', the name (doing business as) of the entity (restaurant)
  • 'BORO', borough in which the entity (restaurant) is located. NOTE: There may be discrepancies between zip code and listed boro due to differences in an establishment's mailing address and physical location
  • 'BUILDING', the building number for the entity (restaurant)
  • 'STREET', the street name at which the entity (restaurant) is located.
  • 'ZIPCODE'
  • 'PHONE'
  • 'CUISINE DESCRIPTION', the entity (restaurant) cuisine.
  • 'INSPECTION DATE', the date of inspection
  • 'ACTION', the actions that is associated with each restaurant inspection.
  • 'VIOLATION CODE', the violation codes that is associated with each restaurant inspection.
  • 'VIOLATION DESCRIPTION', the description that corresponds to the violation codes
  • 'CRITICAL FLAG', indicates if Violation is critical or not.
  • 'SCORE', total Score for a particular inspection
  • 'GRADE',
    • A = Grade A
    • B = Grade B
    • C = Grade C
    • Z = Grade Pending
    • P= Grade Pending issued on re-opening following an initial inspection that resulted in a closure
    • N = Not Yet Graded
  • 'GRADE DATE', the date when the current grade was issued to the entity (restaurant)
  • 'RECORD DATE', the date when the extract was run to produce this data set
  • 'INSPECTION TYPE'], the type of inspection. A combination of the program and inspection type.

Note A single inspection may result in multiple rows if there is more than one violation per inspection, and multiple violations per inspection are common.

Questions for exploratory analysis of our dataset¶

  • What are the most frequently cited violations?
  • How is the makeup of restaurants by district?
  • Overall, what are the popular cuisine types in NYC? How does it vary across the five boroughs of NYC?
  • How does the health grade distribution trend over the years?
  • What are the characteritics of restaurants that are shut down during inspections?

First, we will perform data wrangling and cleaning. Then, we carry out an exploratory analysis of our data.

Summary¶

Our initial analysis gives us a glimpse into frequently cited violations. Restaurant operators could learn to be aware of and watch out for these common violation types and associated penalty scores when striving for the best possible grade.

Manhattan, the third densest borough, has the largest number of restaurants, nearly 40% of the restaurants being located there. Queens and Brooklyn each have nearly 25% shares of restaurants, and with Bronx at 9% and with Staten Island 3%.

American cuisine is the most popular offering in all boroughs, with the top cuisine types slightly varying across boroughs, perhaps reflecting the Boros' ethnicity makeup. Staten Island, which has the highest number of Italian Americans per Borough in the US, Pizza/Italian is an overwhelmingly popular restaurant type at 23% of restaurants in Staten Island offering Pizza/Italian, when compared to other boroughs in NYC where Pizza/Italian tend to make up in percentages ranging from 9% to 13%.

Since 2014 to 2017, percentage of restaurants receiving grade "A" has been steadily increasing, whereas those for grade "B" and "C" dropping at a similar rate. Perhaps, the consumers in New York City are giving greater weight to health grade of restaurants, hence incentivizing restaurant operators to get an A. Nowadays, customer choices of where to dine take place increasingly on online platforms such as Yelp which make previously unnoticeable health grades negatively conspicuous, restaurants may find their businesses increasingly impacted by Bs and Cs.

Another possibility is that the fines for breaking regulation violations in food safety might have been substantially increasing over the past years, since businesses are fined based on the number of violation points. That might be a motivating factor for restaurants to score well in inspections.

When we look at the restaurants that are or have previously been shut down by the city due to health regulation concerns, we see Chinese restaurants are the most common kind to be closed due to health regulations. For the future, it would be good to do more analysis on independent tests between cuisine types and inspection resulting in the restaurant being shut down.
